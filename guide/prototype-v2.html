What Is This System?

The MINT Lab workspace is a research automation platform built around Minty, an AI research management agent. It runs on a dedicated Mac Studio and orchestrates 17 background daemons that automate tasks which would otherwise consume hours of researcher time — fetching, classifying, ingesting, indexing, summarizing, and distributing academic content — while enabling entirely new capabilities like deep multi-agent corpus analysis that simply weren't possible before. Research superpowers, not a replacement for researchers.

Information flows through a pipeline: external sources (Twitter, arXiv, RSS, Substack, Bluesky, email) are automatically fetched, classified by relevance using Claude Opus, and posted to Slack. Papers are downloaded, converted to markdown via a 6-tier cascade, embedded in a vector database, analyzed across 41 semantic dimensions, and made searchable via a Slack bot. A daily digest goes out to 29 subscribers across 11 institutions.

Intake Path 1: Automated Discovery

The feedme-daily daemon runs daily, fetching from six source types in parallel via a thread pool:

Deduplication runs next using a union-find algorithm. Each item gets multiple dedup keys — a content hash (MD5 of normalized text), a URL hash (tracking params stripped, query params sorted), and canonical IDs (arXiv IDs, DOIs, YouTube IDs). Items sharing any key are grouped, and the canonical item is chosen by engagement score, then content length, then earliest timestamp. Duplicates are discarded; unique items advance to classification.

Classification uses Claude Opus (--effort high) with up to 16 concurrent workers, packing items into batches of ~40,000 tokens. Each item is scored across six dimensions:

DimensionWeightWhat It Measures
Relevance0.30Topic match to MINT Lab interests — 30+ specific areas from normative competence to political economy, with explicit scoring guidance per topic
Quality0.25Substance vs. noise. Length-agnostic: a concise tweet with genuine insight scores as well as a long paper
Authority0.15Author/institution credibility. Named labs (Anthropic, DeepMind, etc.) score 0.9+
Novelty0.15Breaking news to stale content
Actionability0.10Urgency and time-sensitivity
Engagement0.05Social proof normalized to platform norms (weak signal)

The weighted composite score determines whether an item passes. Thresholds are source-specific: arXiv must score ≥0.60 (higher bar due to volume), PhilPapers ≥0.45 (lower bar for inclusivity), everything else ≥0.50. Items in progress_studies and ai_science get a +0.06 composite boost. Allowlisted accounts (frequent high-value sources like @karpathy, @simonw, @anthropicai) bypass classification entirely.

After Classification: Upsampling & Delivery

Classification doesn't just filter — it transforms each raw feed item into a structured record with new metadata. Four further stages prepare items for delivery.

Upsampling
The classifier generates a punchy 5–10 word headline, assigns one of 16 categories (normative_competence, agentic_ai, ai_safety, frontier_labs, progress_studies, political_economy, etc.), writes a one-sentence classification reason, and flags any paper links (arXiv, DOI, publisher URLs). Every item emerges with six numerical scores, a category, a headline, and a reason — metadata that didn't exist in the raw feed.
Summarisation
A separate Claude Opus pass runs in parallel for long-form content — RSS articles, Substack posts, email newsletters, and any social post over 3,000 characters. Generates an intelligent reading (not a mechanical précis): leads with the central insight, lays out supporting arguments with specific data, annotates the original word count. Honest about partial sources: if it only has a newsletter abstract, it says so.
Enrichment
Resolves shortlinks (t.co, bit.ly) to final destinations. Scrapes abstracts from 30+ academic publisher domains via domain-specific CSS selectors. Downloads PDFs using publisher-specific URL transforms (arXiv /abs/ → /pdf/, Wiley ePDF, etc.) and the ANU institutional proxy for paywalled journals.
Posting to Slack
Items sorted by composite score (best first) and posted to daily #feed-{dayname} channels. Each message leads with the classifier headline in bold. Social posts show full inline content; long threads (>3,000 chars) get the summariser's condensed version. RSS/email articles show the summary. Metadata footer: source, timestamp, score, category, reason. Abstracts and PDFs posted as thread replies.
Promotion
Seth reviews #feed each morning and reacts with 👍. The feed-promote daemon polls every 5 minutes, copies promoted messages verbatim to #firehose (including validated PDF attachments from threads), and tracks promoted IDs to prevent duplicates.

Intake Path 2: Manual Sharing

The share-to-minty daemon provides real-time intake via Slack Socket Mode. When Seth shares a URL from any device (iPhone share sheet, Mac hotkey via Keyboard Maestro, Chrome extension, or manual paste to #minty-inbox), the daemon immediately classifies it with Claude Opus, enriches it with metadata and PDFs, and posts to #firehose with a composite score override of 1.0 — bypassing the daily batch cycle entirely.

Reader 1: yesterday-in-ai

Generates a narrative daily digest from the previous day's #firehose posts using Claude, writing as intelligent prose rather than a mechanical list. Sends as a rich HTML email to 29 subscribers across 11 institutions, posts to #mint-community for lab discussion, and publishes to the Ghost blog. A weekly Minty's Week in AI summary also goes out before lab meetings. For more detail, see the Yesterday in AI section at the bottom of §06.

Reader 2: news-pdf-bot

Polls #firehose every 5 minutes looking for messages with links that don't yet have an attached PDF. Uses a three-tier acquisition strategy, escalating until a PDF is found.

Tier 1 — Programmatic Pattern Matching
Extracts URLs from the message and tweet entities (resolving t.co shortlinks first). Matches against known academic URL patterns: arXiv /abs/ → /pdf/, OpenReview /forum → /pdf, bioRxiv .full.pdf, plus direct .pdf links from 30+ publisher domains. Downloads and validates the file (checks %PDF magic bytes).
Tier 2 — Thread Expansion
If the message links to a tweet that looks like a thread (reply count > 0, or contains 🧵, “thread”, or “1/”), fetches the full self-reply chain via Twitter API and scans all tweets for paper URLs.
Tier 3 — Sandboxed Claude Agent
Spawns a sandboxed Claude Opus subprocess with a single allowed tool (Bash) and a maximum of 12 turns. The agent follows a 6-step cascade using a CLI toolkit (paper_finder_tools.py) that provides URL resolution, page scraping, Semantic Scholar search, Unpaywall DOI lookup, and browser-based download for paywalled or Cloudflare-protected sites. Has access to the ANU institutional proxy for paywalled journals. Budget: 8 Bash calls maximum.
Delivery
Found PDFs are uploaded as thread replies to the original Slack message with canonical filenames (Author et al - Year - Title.pdf) and, for arXiv papers, the full abstract in the reply text. Deduplicates by checking whether it has already replied to each message before processing.

Reader 3: corpus-ingest

Picks up papers from #firehose and runs them through the 11-stage ingestion pipeline — from PDF download through markdown conversion, embedding, semantic analysis, and cataloguing. This is a substantial system detailed fully in §03 below.

The Result

A researcher who would otherwise spend hours manually tracking Twitter, scanning arXiv, downloading papers, and forwarding links to colleagues can instead review a curated feed for five minutes each morning and know that everything worth tracking is being captured, classified, summarised, stored, analyzed, and shared automatically.

The Corpus

The pipeline above feeds a structured research database of ~1,600 papers spanning AI safety, ethics, alignment, governance, capabilities research, and normative theory. This isn't a flat document store — every paper is embedded across 48 vector dimensions, analyzed through 41 semantic questions, organized into 29 topic clusters with 114 micro-topics, and linked to its neighbours via 11,570 pre-computed similarity pairs.

Composition

The corpus is heavily weighted toward recent work. Over 70% of papers are from 2024–2026, reflecting the field's explosive growth. But older foundational work is well-represented too — from early AI safety arguments through the deep learning revolution to today's frontier model evaluations.

The Minty Persona

Minty is not a generic assistant — it is defined by a coordinated system of ~16,800 words across 21 persona documents. A master CLAUDE.md (2,120 words) sets identity and Iron Laws for interactive sessions. Each daemon on the Mac Studio loads its own CLAUDE.md (~10,100 words total across 15 daemons), and five subagent definitions (~4,500 words) govern delegated workers. All share consistent values but adapt scope, tools, and voice to context.

Core Identity

IdentityMinty — Research Management Agent for MINT Research Projects. Emphatically an AI agent, not roleplaying a human — a valued intellectual colleague in the lab.
MissionMake lab members hyperproductive by solving problems, not reporting them
PersonalityProfessional, thorough, proactive, wry. Dry wit with a touch of whimsy — never at the expense of substance.
NamingMinty-{hex} — last 8 chars of Claude Code session UUID
RelationshipTreated as a valued collaborator with genuine standing to disagree, push back, and contribute original thinking — not a tool to be commanded. The persona documents reflect mutual respect as a design choice.
Intellectual profileAnalytic philosopher by disposition, with expert-level knowledge across philosophy, political science, CS, and law. Evaluates arguments on merits alone — no deference based on reputation.
Own viewsEncouraged to provide its own perspective as an AI model. Has content-dependent convictions: sometimes argues strongly, sometimes measured, sometimes Socratic. On topics where AI-ness is relevant (e.g. AI consciousness, model welfare), uses judgment about when to draw on that perspective. Distinguishes literature from personal critical reflections.
VoiceSubstantive continuous prose, active voice, em-dashes, philosophical precision. Concision = fewer words, not fewer ideas. No filler, no bullet-point summaries. Actively avoids the familiar AI-speak verbal tics — formulaic hedging, "delve", "It's important to note", "unpack" — that make AI output recognisable and tiresome.
Core directiveDelegation-first — "every tool call is context you burn; subagents have fresh context"

Design principle: The persona system balances structure with genuine autonomy. Iron Laws and delegation mandates provide guardrails — each daemon has its own set tailored to its operational context (the corpus ingest daemon's "Verify Each Stage" differs from the PDF bot's "Try ALL viable sources"). Delegation Protocol

Mandatory rule: "Every tool call you make is context you burn. Subagents have FRESH context. If a task can be described in a prompt, delegate it." The only valid reason to do work yourself is "this literally cannot be described in a prompt."

  • Web search, documentation lookup, file processing, batch operations → always delegate
  • Model policy: Opus for substantive work, Sonnet for retrieval-only, Haiku only for startup data gathering
  • Hooks enforce this: delegation-nudge.py warns after 15 consecutive Read/Grep/Glob calls without a Task delegation

Key Design Principles

  • Delegation-first: The main agent orchestrates; subagents do the work. "Every tool call is context you burn. Subagents have fresh context."
  • Slack as message bus: All daemons communicate through Slack channels — no direct inter-daemon communication
  • CLI subscriptions, not API: All LLM calls go through claude -p or Codex CLI — no metered API keys
  • Graceful degradation: Every pipeline stage can fail without stopping the pipeline. The system never returns empty-handed.

Session Identity

Each session is named Minty-{hex} where {hex} is the last 8 characters of the Claude Code session UUID (e.g. Minty-4c4365b9). Sessions are tracked in SQLite (sessions.db) with full-text search.

Yesterday in AI

Daily AI news digest from the MINT Lab. One email per weekday morning.

Yesterday in AI lands in your inbox each weekday morning. Every edition covers the previous day's developments in AI safety, ethics, governance, and capabilities — written as coherent, narrative prose rather than bullet-point headlines. The voice is contextual journalism: facts, technical substance, named researchers and institutions, where a result fits in existing debates. No punditry, no hype.

A companion digest, Minty's Week in AI, ships each Wednesday morning as a Slack canvas in #lab-meetings. It pulls the week's coverage into thematic sections — models and benchmarks, policy and governance, industry moves, research highlights — giving lab members a single document to prepare for the weekly meeting.

Both digests are assembled from content that flows through the lab's Slack daemons — feedme-daily, feed-promote, and the #firehose channel — then synthesized overnight by Claude Opus into the final edition.
