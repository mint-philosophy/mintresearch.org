## Literature Review

### From Vernacular Insult to Contested Concept

"AI slop" entered common parlance not through academic channels but through the pragmatic vocabulary of people confronted daily with content they found disagreeable. The term's emergence traces to at least 2022, but gained real traction when the poet and technologist "deepfates" described it as "the term for unwanted AI generated content" in a widely-shared post. Simon Willison amplified the coinage in May 2024, proposing an analogy that has since become definitive: just as "spam" named the category of unwanted email, "slop" names the category of unwanted AI-generated content. Willison's formulation was carefully scoped — not all AI-generated content is slop, just as not all promotional email is spam. The distinguishing mark is that it is "mindlessly generated and thrust upon someone who didn't ask for it." This framing locates the problem not in the content's provenance (AI-generated) but in the social practice (unsolicited imposition of unreviewed output).

The word's cultural velocity was extraordinary. Merriam-Webster selected "slop" as its 2025 Word of the Year, defining it as "digital content of low quality that is produced usually in quantity by means of artificial intelligence." The American Dialect Society concurred. The lexicographic definition is notably more content-focused than Willison's: it identifies a quality threshold (low), a production method (AI), and a scale condition (quantity), but says nothing about whether the content is solicited or wanted. This divergence between the practice-centred and content-centred framings runs through the entire definitional landscape and has not been resolved.

### Academic Attempts at Definition

Despite the term's ubiquity, formal academic definitions remain sparse. As Kommers et al. (2026) observe in their ACM-published "[Why Slop Matters](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.06060)," AI slop has "so far resisted formal definition." Rather than proposing necessary and sufficient conditions — which they regard as a doomed enterprise for a concept this heterogeneous — they identify three "prototypical properties" in a Wittgensteinian family-resemblance spirit:

1. **Superficial competence**: a veneer of quality belied by deeper lack of substance.
2. **Asymmetry of effort**: creation requires vastly less labour than would be needed without AI.
3. **Mass producibility**: the content exists within a digital ecosystem of widespread generation and consumption.

Kommers et al. also identify three "dimensions of variance" along which instances of slop differ: instrumental utility (why was it created?), personalization (is it targeted or generic?), and surrealism (from ludicrously implausible to deceptively realistic). Notably, the paper argues against treating slop as straightforwardly objectionable — the authors contend it serves legitimate social functions and possesses aesthetic value comparable to other initially-maligned cultural forms.

The other significant academic contribution is Shaib et al. (2025), "[Measuring AI 'Slop' in Text](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.19163)," which takes a more empirical approach. They conducted expert interviews with NLP researchers, writing scholars, and philosophers to develop a dimensional taxonomy for assessing text-based slop, identifying latent dimensions including coherence and relevance. Their contribution is methodological rather than definitional — they provide measurement instruments rather than settling what the concept means.

### Descriptive Framings: What Slop Is

Stripped of evaluative loading — which, as I will argue below, is harder than it sounds — the descriptive core of most definitions converges on a conjunction: AI-generated + low quality + high volume + distributed via engagement-optimized channels. But each element proves slippery under scrutiny.

*AI-generated* seems straightforward but is not. Much online content is AI-augmented rather than wholly AI-produced — edited outputs, AI-assisted drafts, human-curated mashups. The boundary between "AI-generated content" and "content produced with AI tools" is a spectrum, and the term "slop" is typically applied only toward one end of it. Kommers et al.'s "asymmetry of effort" criterion tries to capture this, but effort is both difficult to observe from the outside and normatively loaded (we do not usually think that less effort *per se* degrades output — efficiency is generally good).

*Low quality* is the most obviously evaluative component smuggled into a purportedly descriptive definition. Quality relative to what standard? Merriam-Webster's definition simply asserts "low quality" without specifying the metric. Kommers et al.'s "superficial competence" is more precise: the content *appears* competent but fails on closer inspection. This is a genuinely descriptive observation about a particular failure mode of generative models — they produce fluent, well-formatted output that can be factually wrong, conceptually empty, or vacuously generic. Peterson (2025) provides the technical explanation: LLMs "naturally generate output towards the 'center' of the distribution," so their outputs are systematically biased toward high-frequency patterns and away from the "[long tail](https://drive.google.com/file/d/13s7_fn-vdFY1YQSVUbxCtIQSb59gx9CC/view?usp=drivesdk)" of rarer, more specialised knowledge. This central-tendency property is structural, not accidental — it is a feature of how these models are trained, not a bug that better training will eliminate.

The model collapse literature provides a related technical backstory. Shumailov et al. (2024), discussed by Wenger (2024) in [Nature](https://drive.google.com/file/d/17K0J3e9VUtUoRJ3ucqb8gBNcGXEbwJxK/view?usp=drivesdk), showed that training models recursively on their own output causes progressive forgetting of less-common elements until the models produce nonsense. Gerstgrasser et al. (2024) demonstrated in [their study](https://drive.google.com/file/d/17D2ZyjtQtzJxrgAl-SncY-cIkzqo1Vh8/view?usp=drivesdk) that accumulating synthetic alongside real data avoids collapse, but the underlying point remains: generative models amplify the head and erode the tail. When this process operates not just on model training data but on the broader information environment — when AI outputs become the inputs to human cognition at scale — Peterson (2025) argues it produces "knowledge collapse," a narrowing of the epistemic commons that makes society's beliefs "2.3 times further from the truth" under even a modest (20%) cost discount for AI-generated content.

### Normative Framings: Why Slop Is Objectionable

Most uses of "slop" are not trying to be descriptive. The word itself is pejorative — it derives from "soft mud" and "pig food" — and its deployment is almost invariably an act of condemnation. The interesting question is not whether normative definitions exist (they clearly do) but what the normative objection actually is. Several distinct lines of criticism can be identified.

**Epistemic pollution.** The most developed normative framing treats slop as a form of environmental degradation — not of the physical environment, but of the information ecosystem. Van Rooij (2025) describes AI-generated content infiltrating scientific platforms as "epistemicide," the destruction of knowledge systems themselves. Coeckelbergh (2026) argues in [his analysis](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1OZiuPiu39Y89Q_rRNIw2DJ3SrnwPIozN/view?usp=drivesdk) that AI creates "epistemic laziness" — people accept default machine-generated beliefs rather than engaging in reflective belief revision — and that this diminishes human epistemic agency in ways with deep consequences for personal identity and democratic self-governance. Weatherall and O'Connor (2021) provide the conceptual infrastructure in [their work](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1SG3pVAyZG_mITVDFl5DZTMt4R3Eb_9Hc/view?usp=drivesdk), showing how misleading content succeeds by mimicking legitimate sources, exploiting differential trust dynamics, and leveraging epistemic vices like insouciance.

This epistemic-pollution framing has a structural analogue in Dean's (2009) Foucauldian analysis of power as "[action upon actions](https://drive.google.com/file/d/18t-sXBvmlr7pPlog7vlxhMpHUFLWoXdF/view?usp=drivesdk)." On Dean's "governmental" conception, power operates not through direct coercion but by shaping the field of possible conduct — making certain choices salient, certain information findable, certain discourses available. AI slop, on this reading, is objectionable not because any particular piece of content is false or harmful, but because the *aggregate* reshaping of what is available in the attention environment changes what people can practically think, discuss, and know.

**Automation bias and overreliance.** Cummings (2004) documented in her [foundational study](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1GnrAq3cvrKbGK36ACzFedcEq_iT_pHUw/view?usp=drivesdk) that people systematically over-defer to automated outputs, producing both omission errors (failing to notice when the system is wrong) and commission errors (accepting wrong outputs as correct). Danry et al. (2024) extend this finding to the LLM context in [their experiment](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1ILccitnPW8n2pV4_PKg7OxAiWZgsHDiQ/view?usp=drivesdk) with 23,840 observations: AI-generated deceptive explanations significantly amplify belief in false headlines and undermine true ones, and — crucially — neither cognitive reflection nor trust-in-AI attitudes protect individuals from these effects. The fluency of LLM output is the mechanism: it triggers the same heuristics that make human testimony persuasive, but without the epistemic standing that underwrites testimony's reliability. This connects directly to Kommers et al.'s "superficial competence" — the danger of slop is precisely that it *looks right* while being wrong.

**Illegitimate reason-giving.** Enoch's (2012) analysis of [authority and reason-giving](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1Oj6aSoNC2maQkxggglQ6QnDQMcAJrSwd/view?usp=drivesdk) provides a less-explored but philosophically precise normative lens. Enoch argues that legitimate authority operates through "robust reason-giving" — communicating intentions that give others preemptive reasons to act, backed by appropriate normative conditions. Much AI-generated content presents itself as informative testimony — it invites uptake *as if* it robustly gives reasons — but the success conditions are absent. There is no epistemic standing behind the claims, no accountability for their accuracy, no communicative intention in any meaningful sense. On this view, slop is not merely low-quality content but a systematic misfiring of the reason-giving apparatus that structures epistemic trust.

**Nonconsensual imposition.** Cory Doctorow's (2026) concept of "nonconsensual slopping" locates the wrong not in the content but in the act of sharing: sending unreviewed AI output to strangers constitutes "an attempt to coerce a stranger into unpaid labour" — the labour of evaluating content whose producer did not bother to evaluate it themselves. This framing emphasises the relational dimension — slop wrongs people not because of what it is but because of what it demands from them — and connects to Willison's original emphasis on the practice rather than the product.

### Challenges, Dissent, and Boundary Problems

Not everyone finds "AI slop" analytically useful. D'Isa, writing in The Philosophical Salon, argues that mediocrity has always been the baseline of cultural production — "the machine has simply made our habits explicit" — and that applying different quality standards to machine-generated and human-generated content reflects cultural anxiety rather than philosophical insight. Bechard (2025) in *Scientific American* extends this with historical parallels: chapbooks after the printing press, Grub Street hack-work in the 1700s, nickelodeon films in the 1900s — each media revolution floods the market with cheap content, and each eventually produces both rubbish and art. On this view, slop is a transitional phenomenon, not a stable category.

Kommers et al. (2026) take a middle position — acknowledging slop's existence while resisting the assumption that it is straightforwardly bad. They argue it serves "legitimate social functions" as a cultural-economic solution to demand exceeding human supply capacity, and that dismissing it as mere pollution "obscures important dimensions deserving rigorous examination."

Boundary cases are genuinely difficult. A hobbyist sharing AI-generated images for fun? A student using ChatGPT to draft an email? AI-assisted journalism that saves time but maintains editorial oversight? Eaton's (2007) framework for the pornography debate — in which she insisted on separating the [target category, the hypothesised harms, and the appropriate remedy](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DAtKLrS3t821UE7iI-GoLLSFjXvG_vbB/view?usp=drivesdk) — provides useful discipline here. The term "slop" conflates all three: it simultaneously identifies a category of content, condemns it, and implies a response. Any serious analytical use requires pulling these apart.

Marchal et al. (2024) provide a relevant empirical anchor in [their taxonomy](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1LrRaMgdQ-E6ZD5ob8tr8hgW1PdFxMb13/view?usp=drivesdk) of real-world generative AI misuse. Examining approximately 200 reported incidents, they find that the majority involve "exploitation of easily accessible capabilities requiring minimal technical expertise" — precisely the low-barrier, high-volume production that characterises slop. But their taxonomy also reveals that "manipulation of human likeness and falsification of evidence" are the most prevalent tactics, which cuts against purely quality-based definitions: some of the most harmful AI-generated content is not low-quality at all, but convincingly realistic. Slop and deepfakes are conceptually adjacent but not identical, and the definitional boundaries remain unclear.

## Critical Assessment

The definitional landscape for "AI slop" is fractured in ways that are not merely terminological but reflect genuine disagreement about what — if anything — is distinctively wrong with mass-produced AI content. Let me identify what I think are the key tensions and what a workable definition would need.

**Most definitions are normative even when they claim to be descriptive.** Merriam-Webster's definition presents itself as straightforward lexicography — "digital content of low quality produced usually in quantity by means of artificial intelligence" — but "low quality" is a value judgment, and the choice to define the term at all (as Word of the Year, no less) reflects and reinforces the condemnation built into the vernacular. Kommers et al.'s prototypical properties are more carefully constructed, but "superficial competence" still presupposes a standard against which competence is judged. There is no fully descriptive definition of slop because the concept was born normative. This is not a criticism — many useful concepts (exploitation, corruption, negligence) have this structure — but it needs to be acknowledged rather than disguised.

**The strongest normative framing is structural, not content-level.** The weakest objection to slop is "this content is bad" — bad by whose standard, for what purpose? The strongest objection is that mass-produced, engagement-optimised AI content restructures the attention environment in ways that degrade the epistemic commons. This is the line that connects Peterson's knowledge collapse, Coeckelbergh's epistemic agency erosion, Dean's governmental power, and Cummings's automation bias. The harm is not that any particular piece of AI content fails to meet a quality threshold, but that the *aggregate* — the flood, the sheer volume, the centrality bias, the engagement-optimisation — changes what is practically available to epistemic agents navigating the information environment. Dimitrakakis et al. (2019), while working on [a different problem](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1y_8kyidFmN3MnX2l-Nf6CKQX5RRQwKSf/view?usp=drivesdk), provide a useful warning: any attempt to operationalise a quality threshold for "slop detection" will face uncertainty, distributional shift, and adversarial gaming. The most robust normative definition focuses on the system-level effects rather than content-level properties.

**Eaton's template is the right analytical move.** For the pornography debate, Eaton (2007) showed that progress required (i) narrowing the target category so it does not collapse into "all sexually explicit content," (ii) specifying the harm hypothesis with an explicit causal model, and (iii) separating moral condemnation from the question of remedy. "AI slop" needs exactly the same treatment. The target must be narrower than "all AI-generated content" — it needs to pick out a specific combination of production method, distribution context, and quality failure. The harm hypothesis must specify a causal pathway — not just "this is bad" but "this content, distributed in this way, through this mechanism, produces this harm." And the remedy must be calibrated to the harm, not to the moral revulsion: redesigning ranking algorithms and provenance infrastructure is very different from content bans.

**What is genuinely missing.** The literature has a surfeit of concept-building and a deficit of measurement. Peterson (2025) provides a model, but his simulations assume parameters (e.g., a 20% cost discount) that need empirical grounding. We know from Danry et al. (2024) that AI-generated explanations can amplify belief in misinformation in laboratory settings — but we lack studies of cumulative, diffuse exposure in naturalistic information environments. We know from Shumailov et al. (2024) that recursive AI-on-AI training degrades model quality — but the analogous question for the human epistemic environment (does recursive exposure to AI-generated content degrade human judgment?) remains largely untested. The structural harm hypothesis is plausible and well-theorised, but the empirical programme has barely begun.

**My own view:** "AI slop" is analytically useful as a category if — and only if — it is defined with Eaton-style discipline. The best working definition has a descriptive core and a separable normative overlay:

*Descriptive:* AI slop is model-generated or model-augmented content that exhibits superficial competence (fluent, well-formatted, apparently informative) without corresponding depth (factually unreliable, conceptually generic, or vacuously responsive to prompts), produced cheaply and at scale, and distributed through channels optimised for engagement rather than epistemic quality.

*Normative:* Such content is objectionable when its production and distribution predictably degrade the epistemic environment — raising noise, eroding trust signals, crowding out higher-quality alternatives, and inducing automation-biased overreliance — without adequate accountability, provenance, or justification.

The D'Isa objection — that mediocrity is eternal and slop is just the latest version — has a grain of truth but misses what is genuinely new. The printing press produced Grub Street, but Grub Street writers were at least constrained by the costs of paper, typesetting, and distribution. The marginal cost of producing AI content approaches zero, which removes the economic friction that previously limited the volume of low-quality content. More importantly, AI content is not just cheap to produce but cheap to *personalise* and *optimise for engagement*, creating a feedback loop between production and distribution that has no historical precedent. The mediocrity may be eternal; the scale, the targeting, and the systemic epistemic effects are not.
